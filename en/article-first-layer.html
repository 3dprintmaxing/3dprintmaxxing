First-Layer Problems, Warping, and Failed PLA Prints

Choosing filament is not just a color decision. The material affects strength, flexibility, surface finish, heat resistance, cost, and how forgiving the print will be during production.

If the first layer will not stick

Start with the build plate. Clean it, confirm the correct surface is installed, and remove dust or fingerprints. Run the printer’s leveling or mesh routine. Check that the nozzle is clean and that the first-layer temperature matches the filament profile. A draft near the printer can cool the material too quickly. If the plate is clean and level but adhesion remains poor, test a small first-layer pattern rather than wasting a full model.

If lines are separated

Separated lines usually mean the nozzle is too far from the surface, the bed is uneven, or the extrusion flow is insufficient. Adjust the first-layer height or live Z setting in small increments, following the printer manufacturer’s guidance. Do not compensate for a dirty or loose plate by forcing the nozzle closer. The goal is a continuous layer with gentle contact, not a flattened smear.

If the nozzle drags or the surface looks over-compressed

A nozzle that scrapes can damage the plate and push material into ridges. The nozzle may be too close, the first-layer flow may be too high, or the bed may not be level. Pause the print and inspect the surface. Make a small adjustment, then rerun a first-layer test. If the problem appears only in one area, inspect the plate for warping, debris, or an incorrect mesh.

If corners lift: warping

Warping happens when cooling creates uneven contraction and the part pulls away from the build plate. Clean the plate, reduce drafts, confirm the recommended bed temperature, and improve the first-layer footprint. A brim can help large flat parts. Rounded corners, chamfers, and a slightly different orientation can reduce stress in the design. Enclosing the printer may help some materials, but do not treat an enclosure as a substitute for safe ventilation or correct material guidance.

If the print detaches later

A part can begin well and detach after several layers if the build plate is marginal, the model contracts as it grows, or the environment changes. Check whether the first layer was truly bonded, whether the bed temperature remains stable, and whether the cooling fan turns on too aggressively. Large flat sections and sharp corners are common trouble spots. For a customer print, it is better to pause and request approval for a design adjustment than to deliver a visibly failed or dimensionally distorted part.

If the first layer looks good but the model still fails

Look at the failure height and geometry. A collapse at an overhang points toward supports or orientation. A shift suggests belts, motion, a collision, or the part losing adhesion. A weak wall can indicate flow, temperature, or a design-thickness issue. Stringing may point to moisture or travel settings. The location and timing of the failure are clues; photograph them before removing the print.

A practical troubleshooting order

Use this order: clean the plate, check the nozzle, confirm leveling, inspect the first layer, confirm filament and temperatures, reduce drafts, then adjust one slicer setting. This order avoids spending time on advanced tuning while a basic mechanical or surface problem remains. Keep notes so a successful correction can be repeated on the next print.

When a replacement print is the right answer

Not every failed print is caused by user error. Filament can contain moisture, a part can have an unprintable wall, or a printer can develop a mechanical issue. If a print was ordered from a service and the finished part has a production defect, photograph the issue and contact the service before attempting repairs. The agreed refund or remake policy should determine the next step. Clear photos, the approved model, and the original quote make the discussion faster.
