How to estimate a custom 3D print before requesting a quote

A simple way to think about price is to start with the three details that actually change the job: filament cost, filament weight, and print time. The quote should make those inputs visible instead of hiding them behind unexplained fees.

Start with the material

Choose the filament type and use the current price of a one-kilogram roll. Divide the roll price by 1,000 to get a cost per gram, then multiply by the estimated grams in the model.

Add machine time

Print time matters because the printer is occupied while the job runs. Use the estimated hours as a separate part of the calculation so a small, dense object does not get treated like a large, fast one.

Confirm the details before paying

A final quote should identify the material, estimated weight, estimated print time, and total price. If the model needs a change, ask for a revised quote before approving it.

PLA settings that make beginner prints more reliable

PLA is a forgiving starting material, but reliable results still depend on a clean build plate, sensible temperatures, and a first layer that sticks evenly.

Prepare the plate

Clean the build surface and make sure the printer is level. A strong first layer is more valuable than chasing tiny speed gains.

Use the filament label as a starting point

Begin with the temperature range recommended by the filament maker. Change one setting at a time and keep notes about what improved the result.

Inspect the first layer

Watch the first few minutes. Lines should touch without being scraped flat or left as round, separate strings. Stop early if the first layer is clearly failing.

A quick first-layer and warping troubleshooting guide

Most failed PLA prints reveal their problem early. Checking the first layer before the model gets tall can save material and time.

If corners lift

Clean the plate, reduce drafts, and check the bed temperature recommended for the filament. Large flat parts may also need a brim.

If lines do not touch

Recheck leveling or the printer’s automatic bed-mesh process. A nozzle that is too far from the plate will leave weak, disconnected lines.

If the nozzle drags

The first layer may be too close, or the flow may be too high. Pause the job and correct the setting rather than letting the model continue to fail.
